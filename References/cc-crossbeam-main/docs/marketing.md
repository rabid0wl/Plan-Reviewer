## The Problem: Permits Are Broken

All this demand is crashing into a permitting system that can't keep up. The data validates the crisis from every angle.

### Processing Times Are Brutal

| City | Custom ADU Permit Time | Pre-Approved Plans |
|------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **San Francisco** | 627 days median (multifamily); 861 days (SFR) | 6+ weeks fast track |
| **Los Angeles** | 3-6 months; 260 days in Coastal Zone | 21-30 days |
| **San Diego** | 176 days average residential | 9 days (Affordable Housing Permit Now) |
| **Sacramento** | 4-6 months | Faster |
| **Oakland** | 90-120 days (new detached) | -- |
| **San Jose** | 2-3 months | 4-6 weeks |

SF's median permit approval time has **increased 83% since 2012**. San Diego ADU permits went from 115 days (2020) to 176 days (2024) — getting *worse*, not better. SF's own government website admits the ADU process "may take more than 18 months."

Sources: [SF Chronicle](https://www.sfchronicle.com/sf/article/housing-permits-san-francisco-17652633.php) | [Better Together Builders](https://bettertogetherbuilders.com/adu-permit-timeline-los-angeles-2025/) | [City of San Diego](https://www.sandiego.gov/development-services/permits/timeline) | [SF.gov](https://www.sf.gov/step-by-step--adu-process-timeline) | [Terner Center](https://ternercenter.berkeley.edu/blog/adus-coastal-zone/)

### Rejection Rates Are Sky-High

- **San Jose:** ADU applications sent back for missing information **more than 90% of the time** — [Mercury News](https://www.mercurynews.com/2025/09/05/artificial-intelligence-san-jose-permitting-process/)
- **San Diego County:** 50%+ of projects require more than 2 plan check cycles — [SD County PDS](https://www.sandiegocounty.gov/pds/docs/pds441a.pdf)
- **Los Angeles:** Most projects require **2-3 correction cycles** before approval — [JDJ Consulting](https://jdj-consulting.com/how-to-read-ladbs-correction-notices-a-homeowners-guide-to-plan-check-comments/)
- **Sacramento:** 38% of ADU applications returned for corrections at least once — [Fortune ADU](https://www.fortuneadu.com/blogs/post/how-to-fast-track-an-adu-permit-in-sacramento-county-without-delays)
- Pre-approved plans have **near-zero corrections** — [CalBuild Remodel](https://www.calbuildremodel.com/post/los-angeles-adu-guide-late-2025-60-day-fast-track-permits-the-zoning-code-overhaul)

These are exactly the kind of "spell-check-level" issues CrossBeam is designed to catch: missing signatures, absent stamps, overlooked code citations, incomplete forms, and missing documents.

## The Regulatory Tailwind

California has passed **25+ ADU bills since 2016**, systematically tearing down barriers. The state is practically begging for more ADUs — and punishing cities that drag their feet.

### Key Legislative Timeline

| Year | Bills | What Changed |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 2016 | SB 1069, AB 2299 | Relaxed parking, eased fees, created JADUs |
| 2017 | SB 229, AB 494 | 60-day ministerial review mandate |
| 2019 | AB 68, AB 881, SB 13 | ADU + JADU on any lot; killed parking replacement; waived impact fees <750 sq ft; allowed on multifamily; voided restrictive HOA covenants |
| 2022 | SB 897, AB 2221 | 16-18 ft height limits; two-story ADUs allowed |
| 2023 | AB 1033, AB 976, AB 434, AB 1332 | ADU condo sales; permanent no owner-occupancy; pre-approved plans required statewide by Jan 2025; 30-day approval for pre-approved |
| 2025 | SB 9, SB 543, AB 462, AB 130 | HCD can void non-compliant ordinances; both attached + detached ADU allowed; deemed-approved for coastal; CEQA reform for infill |

Sources: [BBK Law](https://bbklaw.com/resources/la-110725-governor-newsom-signs-four-new-accessory-dwelling-unit-bills) | [CA YIMBY](https://cayimby.org/legislation/2025-sb-9/) | [BWS Law](https://www.bwslaw.com/insights/public-law-update-2025-adu-legislative-update/) | [Loeb & Loeb](https://www.loeb.com/en/insights/publications/2026/01/2025-california-housing-legislative-update-updated-jan-8-2026)

### Enforcement Is Getting Real

- **SB 9 (2025):** HCD can now **void non-compliant local ordinances entirely**. State law becomes the default.
- **$10,000-$50,000/month penalties** per violation for cities that obstruct — [CALBO](https://www.calbo.org/post/permitting-timelines)
- **LA, Pasadena, Glendale, Palo Alto, Dana Point** all received formal HCD non-compliance findings in 2024-2025 — [HCD Accountability](https://www.hcd.ca.gov/planning-and-community-development/accountability-and-enforcement)
- **Glendale:** HCD found 15+ violations, declared ordinance null and void, entered formal MOU in Oct 2025 — [HCD Glendale](https://www.hcd.ca.gov/sites/default/files/docs/policy-and-research/ordinance-review-letters/glendale-adu-follow-up-nov-081525.pdf)

The message is clear: the state wants more ADUs, faster. Cities that resist are getting called out and fined. But the bottleneck isn't willful obstruction — it's capacity. Cities literally don't have enough staff to process the volume.

### Where CrossBeam Is Different

The funded competitors are mostly **proprietary SaaS platforms** selling to cities or large builders. CrossBeam's differentiation:

1. **Open source** — Cities can adopt without procurement cycles or vendor lock-in
2. **Corrections letter interpreter** — No competitor is specifically helping contractors understand and respond to corrections letters. This is a unique, underserved pain point.
3. **Contractor-first** — Most competitors sell to cities (government-side). CrossBeam serves the contractor/homeowner side.
4. **Address-based checklist generation** — Proactive "here's what you need before you submit" vs. reactive "here's what's wrong after you submitted"
5. **Hackathon-born, community-driven** — Not trying to be a VC-funded startup; trying to be infrastructure

## The Bottom Line

**The thesis holds.** California has a permit crisis, and it's getting worse even as the state passes law after law trying to fix it. The core problem isn't political will — the legislature and governor are aggressively pro-ADU. The problem is **execution capacity** at the city level: not enough staff, too much complexity, and a system that punishes good-faith contractors with 2-3 rounds of corrections on issues that could be caught before submission.

CrossBeam sits in the gap between what the state wants (more ADUs, faster) and what cities can deliver (understaffed departments drowning in applications). The $250M+ in VC deployed to this space confirms the opportunity is real. The open-source, contractor-first approach is unoccupied territory.

**Three numbers that tell the whole story:**
1. **429,503** ADU permits issued in California since 2018
2. **90%+** of ADU applications in San Jose sent back for missing information
3. **$30,000** — what a 6-month permit delay costs a homeowner

CrossBeam exists to close that gap.
